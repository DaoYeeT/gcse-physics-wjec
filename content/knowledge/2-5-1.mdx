# 2-5-1 Solar system, stellar evolution and H-R diagram

**(Higher only)** Hertzsprung-Russell diagram interpretation is expected at higher.

**Key facts**
- Solar System order: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars (terrestrial), Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune (gas/ice giants). Dwarf planets (e.g. Pluto), comets, asteroids and moons.
- Distance units: km (within Solar System), AU (Sun-Earth distance, $\sim 1.5\times 10^{11}$ m), light-year (l-y, $\sim 9.46\times 10^{15}$ m, interstellar).
- **Low/medium-mass star life cycle**: nebula, protostar, main sequence, red giant, planetary nebula and white dwarf (which fades to black dwarf).
- **High-mass star life cycle**: nebula, protostar, main sequence, red supergiant, supernova, neutron star or black hole.
- A main-sequence star is stable because of the balance between inward gravitational collapse and outward pressure from fusion radiation and gas.
- Supernovae scatter heavy elements into space; these later form new stars and planets.
- **Hertzsprung-Russell diagram**: luminosity on the y-axis (increasing up), surface temperature on the x-axis (decreasing right). Main sequence is a diagonal band from top-left (hot/bright) to bottom-right (cool/dim). Red giants top-right; white dwarfs bottom-left.

**Key equations**
- $\text{speed} = \text{distance} / \text{time}$ (e.g. light-year as time-to-distance conversion)

**Common misconceptions**
- "Light year is a unit of time" — it is a unit of distance.
- "All stars become black holes" — only the most massive do.

**Worked example**
A galaxy is 10 million light-years away. When did the light reaching us now leave the galaxy?

Light takes 10 million years to arrive, so it left 10 million years ago.
