# 2-1-1 Kinematics, motion graphs and stopping distance

**Key facts**
- **Speed** is scalar (distance/time). **Velocity** is a vector (displacement/time, with direction).
- **Acceleration** $= \Delta v / t$. Deceleration is negative acceleration.
- On a distance-time graph, gradient $=$ speed.
- On a velocity-time graph, gradient $=$ acceleration; area under the line $=$ distance travelled. **(Higher only)**
- **Thinking distance** $=$ speed $\times$ reaction time (typical reaction time ~0.6-0.7 s).
- **Braking distance** depends on speed, road conditions, brake condition and vehicle mass.
- **Stopping distance** $=$ thinking distance $+$ braking distance.
- Factors that increase thinking distance: alcohol, drugs, tiredness, distraction. Factors that increase braking distance: wet/icy road, worn tyres/brakes, heavier load.

**Key equations**
- $\text{speed} = \text{distance} / \text{time}$
- $a = \Delta v / t$
- Distance $=$ area under the $v$-$t$ graph **(Higher only)**

**Common misconceptions**
- "Speed and velocity are the same" — velocity needs direction.
- "Braking distance doubles when speed doubles" — it actually quadruples ($\propto v^2$).

**Worked example**
A car at 20 m/s; reaction time 0.7 s; deceleration 5 m/s$^2$ while braking. Total stopping distance?

Thinking $= 20 \times 0.7 = 14$ m.

Braking, using $v^2 = u^2 + 2ax$ with $v = 0$, $u = 20$, $a = -5$:

$$0 = 400 - 10x \Rightarrow x = 40\ \text{m}$$

Total $= 14 + 40 = 54$ m.
