# 2-7-1 Atomic structure, alpha, beta and gamma radiation

**(Higher only)** Balancing nuclear equations using full notation is higher-only.

**Key facts**
- The atom has a dense positive nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by orbiting electrons.
- **Nucleon (mass) number** $A =$ protons + neutrons; **proton number** $Z =$ protons (defines the element).
- **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
- Radioactive decay is **random** and spontaneous; one cannot predict which atom decays when.
- Background radiation: natural (radon from rocks/soil, cosmic rays, food, internal K-40) plus artificial (medical X-rays, fallout). Radon varies regionally.
- **Alpha** ($\alpha$): a helium nucleus, 2 protons + 2 neutrons; stopped by paper / skin / a few cm of air; very ionising; dangerous if inhaled or swallowed.
- **Beta** ($\beta$): a high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus when a neutron becomes a proton; stopped by ~3 mm of aluminium; moderately ionising.
- **Gamma** ($\gamma$): high-energy electromagnetic wave; stopped by thick lead/concrete; least ionising per metre but most penetrating.

**Key equations**
Nuclear equations balance both mass number and proton number:

- Alpha decay: $^{A}_{Z}X \to {^{A-4}_{Z-2}Y} + {^{4}_{2}He^{2+}}$
- Beta decay: $^{A}_{Z}X \to {^{A}_{Z+1}Y} + {^{0}_{-1}e}$

**Common misconceptions**
- "Beta particles come from the electron cloud" — they come from the nucleus (neutron becomes proton + electron + antineutrino).
- "Gamma decay changes the element" — gamma is just energy emission; $A$ and $Z$ are unchanged.
- "Background radiation can be eliminated" — it can be minimised but never eliminated.

**Worked example**
$^{238}_{92}U \to {^{234}_{90}Th} + ?$ — identify the decay particle.

Mass: $238 - 234 = 4$. Proton: $92 - 90 = 2$. So the particle is $^{4}_{2}He^{2+}$, i.e. an alpha particle.
