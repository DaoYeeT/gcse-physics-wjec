# 1-6-1 Critical angle, optical fibres and medical imaging

**(Higher only)** Comparison of endoscopy with CT scanning is expected at higher depth.

**Key facts**
- Total internal reflection (TIR) occurs when light travels from a denser medium toward a less dense medium AND the angle of incidence exceeds the **critical angle**.
- Optical fibres are thin strands of glass; light bounces along by repeated TIR.
- Advantages of fibres over satellites: lower error rate, more secure, higher data rate, no atmospheric interference.
- Disadvantages of fibres: require physical cable; expensive to lay submarine cables.
- **Endoscopy**: bundles of optical fibres plus a camera carry visible light into body cavities; non-ionising; real-time imagery; cannot image through bone.
- **CT scan**: uses X-rays; can image bone and other hard structures but X-rays are ionising and carry a small cancer risk.

**Key equations**
- $\text{speed} = \text{distance} / \text{time}$ (used for signal-delay calculations)

**Common misconceptions**
- "TIR happens for any angle if there is a denser medium" — only for angles greater than the critical angle.
- "Optical fibres carry no signal loss" — they have small attenuation; signals are re-amplified over long distances.

**Worked example**
An optical fibre between Wales and the USA is 9000 km long. Light in glass travels at $c/2 = 1.5\times 10^8$ m/s. Find the signal delay.

$$t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{9\times 10^6}{1.5\times 10^8} = 0.06\ \text{s} = 60\ \text{ms}$$
