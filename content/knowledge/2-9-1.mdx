# 2-9-1 Fission, fusion and reactor design

**(Higher only)** Quantitative balancing of nuclear equations and discussion of containment sit at higher tier in practice.

**Key facts**
- **Fission**: a heavy nucleus (e.g. $^{235}_{92}U$) absorbs a slow neutron, becomes unstable and splits into two smaller "daughter" nuclei plus 2-3 neutrons and a large release of energy.
- The released neutrons can trigger further fissions, giving a **chain reaction**. Controlled in a reactor; uncontrolled in a nuclear bomb.
- A typical fission reaction: $^{235}_{92}U + {^{1}_{0}n} \to {^{141}_{56}Ba} + {^{92}_{36}Kr} + 3\, {^{1}_{0}n}$.
- **Moderator** (water or graphite): slows fast neutrons to thermal speeds so they can be absorbed by U-235.
- **Control rods** (boron, cadmium): absorb neutrons; lowered or raised to control the rate of reaction.
- Fission products are themselves radioactive, often with long half-lives — long-term storage problem.
- **Fusion**: light nuclei (e.g. $^{2}_{1}H + {^{3}_{1}H} \to {^{4}_{2}He} + {^{1}_{0}n}$) combine, releasing huge energy. Powers stars.
- Fusion challenges: extreme temperature ($\sim 10^8$ K) needed for nuclei to overcome electrostatic repulsion; plasma must be confined (magnetic confinement, e.g. tokamak); no commercial fusion reactor yet.

**Key equations**
- Balance nuclear equations: mass numbers and proton numbers conserved on both sides.

**Common misconceptions**
- "Fission and fusion are the same" — fission splits heavy nuclei; fusion combines light nuclei.
- "Fusion is dirty" — fusion produces far less long-lived radioactive waste than fission.
- "A nuclear reactor can explode like a bomb" — modern designs cannot reach the geometry or enrichment needed for a nuclear explosion; meltdowns release radioactivity but are not nuclear explosions.

**Worked example**
Complete: $^{235}_{92}U + {^{1}_{0}n} \to {^{94}_{38}Sr} + {^{140}_{54}Xe} + n\, {^{1}_{0}n}$.

Mass: $235 + 1 = 94 + 140 + n \Rightarrow n = 2$. Proton: $92 + 0 = 38 + 54 + 0$.

So 2 neutrons are released.
