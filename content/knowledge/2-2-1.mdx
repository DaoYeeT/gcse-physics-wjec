# 2-2-1 Forces, inertia, weight and terminal velocity

**Key facts**
- **Inertia** is a body's tendency to keep doing what it is doing — measured by mass.
- **Newton's 1st law**: an object stays at rest or at constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force.
- **Newton's 2nd law**: $F = ma$. The same resultant force gives a smaller acceleration to a more massive body.
- **Newton's 3rd law**: every action has an equal and opposite reaction; forces act in pairs on different bodies.
- **Weight** $= mg$ is a force; **mass** is a property. On Earth $g \approx 10$ N/kg.
- Falling through air: weight pulls the object down; air resistance increases with speed. When air resistance equals weight, the resultant is zero and the object travels at constant **terminal velocity**. Opening a parachute increases air resistance suddenly, so the object decelerates to a new lower terminal velocity.

**Key equations**
- $F = ma$
- $W = mg$

**Common misconceptions**
- Confusing mass (kg) and weight (N).
- "If forces are balanced, the object stops" — by Newton's 1st law it continues at constant velocity.
- "The 3rd law says forces cancel" — they act on different bodies, so they do not cancel.

**Worked example**
A 70 kg parachutist falls. Just after jumping (negligible air resistance), what is the acceleration? At terminal velocity what is the resultant force?

Just after jumping: $W = 70 \times 10 = 700$ N down, so $a = F/m = 10$ m/s$^2$ (the value of $g$).

At terminal velocity: resultant $= 0$ N (air resistance up balances weight down).
