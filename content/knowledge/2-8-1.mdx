# 2-8-1 Activity, half-life and uses of radioisotopes

**Key facts**
- **Half-life** $T_{1/2}$ is the time taken for the number of undecayed nuclei (or count rate / activity) to halve.
- After $n$ half-lives, remaining fraction $= (1/2)^n$. So after 3 half-lives, $1/8$ remain.
- Half-life is determined from a decay curve (count rate vs time) by reading off the time for any activity to drop to half its value.
- Different isotopes have characteristic half-lives ranging from microseconds to billions of years (e.g. C-14: 5730 years; U-238: $4.5\times 10^9$ years; I-131: 8 days).
- Typical uses combine half-life, penetrating power and biological effect:
  - **Medical tracers**: short half-life, gamma (penetrates body, easily detected).
  - **Cancer treatment**: gamma (penetrates to tumour) or beta (surface skin treatments).
  - **Smoke alarms**: alpha source (Am-241, $T_{1/2} \approx 432$ y) — long half-life gives steady output.
  - **Carbon dating**: organic remains less than ~60 000 years old.

**Key equations**
- No new equation; iterative halving: $A = A_0 \times (1/2)^n$ where $n = t / T_{1/2}$.

**Common misconceptions**
- "After 2 half-lives the source has fully decayed" — $1/4$ remains.
- "Half-life means an atom decays after that time" — decay is random; it is a statistical average.
- Forgetting to subtract background count rate before plotting the decay curve.

**Worked example**
A source has an initial net activity of 800 Bq above background (20 Bq). After 12 minutes the count rate (including background) is 220 Bq. Find the half-life.

Net activity at 12 min $= 220 - 20 = 200$ Bq. Going $800 \to 400 \to 200$ takes 2 half-lives.

$$T_{1/2} = 12 / 2 = 6\ \text{minutes}$$
