# 1-7-1 P-waves, S-waves and the Earth's interior

**Key facts**
- **P-waves**: primary, longitudinal, fastest, travel through solid and liquid, arrive first at a seismograph.
- **S-waves**: secondary, transverse, slower, travel through solid only.
- **Surface waves** travel along Earth's surface and cause most damage.
- The **lag time** between P and S arrivals on a seismograph gives the distance to the epicentre (greater lag means further away).
- **Three or more** seismic stations allow triangulation to pinpoint the epicentre.
- The **S-wave shadow zone** — a band on the opposite side of Earth where S-waves are not detected — shows that the outer core is liquid (or behaves as a liquid to S-waves).

**Key equations**
- $\text{speed} = \text{distance} / \text{time}$

**Common misconceptions**
- "P stands for pressure, S stands for stress" — P is for **primary** (first to arrive) and S for **secondary**.
- "S-waves cannot travel through any liquid in the Earth" — confirmed: the outer core is liquid but the solid mantle and solid inner core do transmit S-waves.

**Worked example**
A P-wave (8 km/s) and an S-wave (4 km/s) leave the same epicentre. The lag time at a station is 120 s. Find the distance.

Time difference: $t_S - t_P = d/4 - d/8 = d/8$.

$$\frac{d}{8} = 120 \Rightarrow d = 960\ \text{km}$$
