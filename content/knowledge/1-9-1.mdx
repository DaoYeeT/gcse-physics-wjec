# 1-9-1 Motor effect, induction and transformers

**(Higher only)** $F = BIl$ quantitatively and $V_1/V_2 = N_1/N_2$ quantitatively (foundation gets qualitative transformer only).

**Key facts**
- A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field — use the right-hand grip rule.
- **Fleming's left-hand rule** (motor effect): First finger Field (N to S), seCond finger Current (+ to $-$), thuMb Motion (force); all mutually perpendicular.
- A simple d.c. motor uses a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field; the **commutator** reverses the current every half turn so the coil keeps rotating in the same direction.
- To increase motor speed: more current, stronger magnets, more turns on the coil, soft-iron core.
- **Electromagnetic induction**: a changing magnetic flux through a conductor induces an EMF — moving wire in a field, or a changing field around a fixed wire.
- **A.c. generator**: a coil rotated in a magnetic field; brushes and slip rings deliver alternating current.
- **Transformer**: two coils on a soft-iron core. A changing current in the primary creates a changing flux that induces an EMF in the secondary. Step-up: more turns on secondary; step-down: fewer turns.

**Key equations**
- $F = BIl$ (force on a current-carrying conductor at right angles to a field) **(Higher only)**
- $V_1/V_2 = N_1/N_2$ (ideal transformer)

**Common misconceptions**
- "A transformer works with d.c." — it requires a changing current, so a.c.
- Mixing up Fleming's **left** (motor) and **right** (generator) hand rules.

**Worked example**
A wire of length 0.20 m carries 3.0 A perpendicular to a 0.5 T field. Find the force.

$$F = BIl = 0.5 \times 3.0 \times 0.20 = 0.30\ \text{N}$$
