# 1-8-1 Gas laws, specific heat and latent heat

**(Higher only)** $pV/T = \text{constant}$, kelvin conversion, and the molecular-motion explanation of gas pressure are higher-only.

**Key facts**
- Pressure $=$ force per area (Pa or N/m$^2$).
- Gas molecules move randomly and exert pressure by colliding with container walls.
- Heating a gas means molecules move faster, giving harder and more frequent collisions, so higher pressure at fixed volume (or larger volume at fixed pressure).
- Compressing a gas reduces the volume so collisions are more frequent and pressure rises (same number of molecules in less space).
- **Absolute zero** $= -273\ ^{\circ}\text{C} = 0\ \text{K}$. Particles have minimum (effectively zero) kinetic energy.
- $T/\text{K} = \theta/^{\circ}\text{C} + 273$ — always convert to kelvin before using $pV/T$.
- Specific heat capacity $c$ is the energy needed to raise 1 kg by 1 $^{\circ}$C.
- Specific latent heat $L$ is the energy needed to change the state of 1 kg with no temperature change.

**Key equations**
- $p = F/A$
- $pV/T = \text{constant}$ (**Higher only**, $T$ in kelvin)
- $\Delta Q = m c \Delta\theta$
- $Q = mL$

**Common misconceptions**
- "Particles in a solid don't move" — they vibrate around fixed positions.
- "Heat and temperature are the same" — temperature is average KE per particle; heat is total energy transferred.
- Forgetting to convert $^{\circ}$C to K in gas-law calculations.

**Worked example**
A gas of volume 1.0 L at 27 $^{\circ}$C and 100 kPa is heated to 327 $^{\circ}$C at constant volume. Find the new pressure.

$T_1 = 300$ K, $T_2 = 600$ K. At constant $V$, $p_1/T_1 = p_2/T_2$.

$$p_2 = p_1 \times \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 100 \times \frac{600}{300} = 200\ \text{kPa}$$
