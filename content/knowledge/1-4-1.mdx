# 1-4-1 Mains electricity, safety devices and energy cost

**Key facts**
- The **kilowatt (kW)** is power; the **kilowatt-hour (kWh)** is energy ($1\ \text{kWh} = 3.6\ \text{MJ}$).
- **A.c.** reverses direction periodically (UK mains: 230 V, 50 Hz); **d.c.** flows in one direction (batteries).
- The **ring main** is the standard UK domestic circuit; sockets are connected in a ring so each has two paths back to the consumer unit.
- The **live** wire carries 230 V a.c.; the **neutral** is at ~0 V; the **earth** is a safety wire that carries fault current to ground.
- A **fuse** is a thin wire that melts when current exceeds its rating (typical UK plug fuses: 3 A, 5 A, 13 A).
- An **MCB** (miniature circuit breaker) trips magnetically or thermally and is resettable.
- An **RCCB** detects an imbalance between live and neutral currents (current leaking to earth, e.g. through a person) and trips within milliseconds — protects against electric shock.

**Key equations**
- $\text{units (kWh)} = P\,(\text{kW}) \times t\,(\text{h})$
- $\text{cost} = \text{units} \times \text{cost per unit}$
- $P = VI$ (used for choosing a fuse rating just above the operating current)

**Common misconceptions**
- "Fuses are to stop appliances overheating" — they break the circuit if current is too high, preventing fire or electrocution.
- "The earth wire normally carries current" — only in a fault condition.

**Worked example**
A 2.3 kW kettle on a 230 V supply. Which standard fuse should be fitted?

$$I = \frac{P}{V} = \frac{2300}{230} = 10\ \text{A}$$

Choose the next standard rating above 10 A — a **13 A** fuse.
