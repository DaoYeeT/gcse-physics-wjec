# 1-2-1 Generation, the National Grid and energy resources

**Key facts**
- **Renewable sources**: hydroelectric, wind, wave, tidal, biomass (waste/crops/wood), solar.
- **Non-renewable sources**: fossil fuels (coal, gas, oil) and nuclear.
- In a fuel-based power station: fuel is burned, heats water, steam drives a turbine, the turbine turns a generator (electromagnetic induction).
- **Sankey diagrams** show energy in, energy usefully transferred, and energy wasted.
- The **National Grid** distributes electricity using step-up transformers near power stations (high voltage, low current, low resistive heating losses) and step-down transformers near homes.
- Comparison criteria for power stations include efficiency, reliability, carbon footprint (CO2 per kWh) and output.

**Key equations**
- $\%\ \text{efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful energy out}}{\text{total energy in}} \times 100$
- $P = VI$ (transmission: for the same $P$, larger $V$ gives smaller $I$ and lower $I^2 R$ losses)

**Common misconceptions**
- "Electricity is created" — energy is transferred; it cannot be created.
- "High-voltage transmission is to push electricity further" — it is because $P_{loss} = I^2 R$ in cables, so reducing $I$ dramatically reduces loss for the same delivered power.

**Worked example**
A power station delivers 20 MW. The transmission cable has resistance 0.5 $\Omega$. Compare losses at 11 kV vs 400 kV.

At 11 kV: $I = P/V = 20\times10^6 / 11000 \approx 1818\ \text{A}$, so $P_{loss} = I^2 R \approx 1.65\ \text{MW}$ (~8% lost).

At 400 kV: $I = 20\times10^6 / 400000 = 50\ \text{A}$, so $P_{loss} = 50^2 \times 0.5 = 1250\ \text{W}$ (~0.006% lost).
